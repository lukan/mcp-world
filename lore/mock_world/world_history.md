# The Ember Inn and the Town of Ashenveil
*As recorded in the travel notes of Maret Voss, merchant, third year of the Quiet Reign*

---

## Ashenveil

A small town at the southern mouth of Grayfen Pass, Ashenveil grew up around a waystation that predates it by perhaps a century. The town exists because the road exists. The road runs north through the pass and, once, connected to the trade routes beyond the Grayfen ridge — a dozen settlements, a mining operation, and the seasonal market at Vel Dura that drew merchants from three provinces.

That was before.

Ashenveil has perhaps four hundred people in good seasons, fewer in winter. It has a blacksmith, a mill, a small temple to no particular god, and the Ember Inn. The inn is the oldest structure still standing. The people who live in Ashenveil are not especially friendly to strangers but they are not hostile. They have learned to wait and see.

---

## The Ember Inn

The innkeeper is a woman named Hadra. She has run the Ember Inn for twenty years, inheriting it from her uncle, who inherited it from his mother, who built the east wing herself. The fire in the common room has been burning continuously for longer than anyone can verify — there is a story that it has not gone out in sixty years, which is probably not true, but nobody at the inn will deny it.

Hadra keeps rooms for travelers. She is known, among those who know her, for holding rooms past the agreed date when a regular guest is overdue. She does not advertise this. She does not discuss it. If you ask how long she will hold a room, she will look at you for a moment before answering with something that is not quite an answer.

The common room is warm. The food is reliable. Hadra does not encourage conversation but she does not refuse it.

---

## The North Road

The trade road through Grayfen Pass has been effectively closed for eight months, though no authority has declared it so. It is closed the way a thing becomes closed when enough people stop using it — gradually, then completely, then with a kind of collective agreement that is never spoken aloud.

Three merchant parties went north last autumn. None returned.

A fourth group — seven men, organized by the Ashenveil merchants' council — went north to look for them. Four came back. The three who did not are not discussed by name in Ashenveil. The four who returned spent one week at the Ember Inn. They did not speak much. When they left, they went south.

Since then, the road north has carried almost no traffic. A few people have gone — travelers who did not know, or who knew and went anyway. The town does not stop them. There is no law against it. There is only the saying that has become common in Ashenveil over the past months:

*The road north takes what it wants.*

No one can tell you exactly where this saying came from. It feels older than it is.

---

## Kesrath

At the head of the pass, where the road crests the ridge before descending into the territory beyond, there is a watchtower called Kesrath's Lookout. It was built three hundred years ago by a warden named Kesrath, who kept it for forty years and is buried in its foundation, as was the custom for wardens in that period.

The tower was decommissioned two centuries ago. It has stood empty since — or was believed to be empty.

The four men who came back from the pass did not say much about what they found. But one of them, on the last night before they left the inn, said something to Hadra that she has not repeated to anyone. Whatever it was, she stopped asking whether the north road would reopen.

The tower appears on maps. The name Kesrath appears on maps. In Ashenveil, people do not say the name out loud if they can avoid it. This is recent. A year ago, no one thought about it at all.

---

## What Is Known

The following is known:

Someone or something has been in the tower.

Whatever happened to the merchant parties is not known.

The road is passable in terms of terrain. There is nothing physically blocking it.

People who go north sometimes do not come back.

People who come back do not explain why others did not.

Hadra still holds rooms for guests who are overdue. She has not said how long she will keep holding them. If you push her on it, she will change the subject, and she will not meet your eyes when she does it.

---

*These notes were written at the Ember Inn, Ashenveil, during a stay of four days while I waited for conditions to improve. I did not take the north road. I am not sure I would call that wisdom, exactly. But I am still here, and the notes are still here, which is something.*

*— M.V.*
